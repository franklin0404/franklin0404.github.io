Learning Physics with Graph Neural Networks

Abstract

Numerical methods have been successful in simulating physical quantities over time. For instance, given the governing equation, an N-body simulation evolves the position, velocity, and acceleration of each particle at given time steps. N-body simulations have reproduced many astrophysical phenomena, such as the non-linear structure formation in the universe. However, it often requires many trial-and-errors for tuning the parameters in the governing equations for the simulation to reproduce astrophysical phenomena. Thus, it is important to ask whether we can learn the parameters of governing equations purely by the data we can observe. Following Cranmer et al. (2020), I tried to recover the governing physics of N-body simulations by looking at the trajectories of particles with the aid of a Graph Neural Network (GNN). In this work, I achieved the goals below: (1) Reimplement Cranmer et al. (2020) from scratch in Julia. (2) Found how noise affects GNN.

(Lecture) Protein Structure Prediction with Graph Neural Network

Proteins are vital to all known living organisms as they perform many complex functions which involve growth, reaction to stimuli, metabolism, energy transformation, and reproduction. The function of proteins was found to be majorly determined by their structure, while the structure of the protein was suspected to depend on the sequence of amino acids of the protein. Nonetheless, people have had been struggled to predict protein structures by their sequence for nearly 50 years until 2021, when AlphaFold 2 accurately predicts the protein structure with an uncertainty lower than the experimental uncertainty. The novel approach in AlphaFold 2 can also be applied to other studies such as the prediction of transcription factor binding sites on DNA. Please see the materials here for more details.

Inspect Autism Spectrum Disorder from DNA Sequence

Abstract

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a developmental disability caused by differences in the brain. Thus, people usually attribute the cause of ASD to mutations of neuron-related gene expression. However, unlike other genetic diseases, ASD is caused by simultaneous mutations in multiple genes, including the genes which regulate synapse function, chromatin regulation, transcriptional regulation, and so on. Therefore, determining the mutation causing ASD is a highly complex task. Fortunately, parents who carry ASD-causing mutations do not necessarily pass their mutation to their children. By comparing the DNA sequences of ASD-carrying and non-ASD-carrying family members, we are able to diagnose the combinations of mutations that cause ASD. Thanks to the DNA sequences obtained from the lab at the Graduate Institute of Medical Genomics and Proteomics, my colleagues and I were able to identify the mutations that cause ASD.

Magic Cube Solver                                                              

Abstract

This is a magic cube solving robot prototype built by my colleagues and me. We demonstrated the robot at the NTU Azalea Festival and attracted hundreds of attendees to visit the booth. I majorly designed a program to identify the colors of the magic cube through a web camera using python and JAVA, while I also helped design and 3D print the hardware and control system. Please see the demonstration on YouTube.
